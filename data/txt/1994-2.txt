Fra redaktøren

Forrige nummer av TG var nettopp gått i trykken da meldingen om Erling Sverdrup's

bortgang kom. Med dette er grunnleggeren av det norske matematisk statistiske miljøet
borte. Det jeg sa i forrige leder fikk med dette dobbelt vekt: Nå er vår generasjon nødt
til å overta hele ansvaret.

Vi overtar i en verden som ikke er blitt lettere, heller ikke for statistikere. Et spesielt

problem for oss i dag er den utfordringen som ligger i forhold til andre miljøer som
også driver dataanalyse og metodeutvikling. Nevral nettverk-miljøet har tidligere vært
diskutert i TG av Knut Liestøl (desember 1992). Det som gjøres innenfor dette miljøet,
er formulert på en for oss uvant måte ut fra noder og forbindelser mellom noder, men i
virkeligheten arbeider de med metoder som konkurrerer med regresjon, diskriminantanalyse, klusteranalyse osv.. Spesielt interessant er språkbruken: De snakker om
læring" der vi snakker om 'estimering', om 'pruning', som inneholder det vi kaller
'variabelutvelgelse' i multippel regresjon, osv.

En del av de beste innenfor dette feltet er opptatt av kontakt med statistikere. Ordet

'generalisering' er kommet mer i forgrunnen innenfor nevrale nett, og det dekker mye
av det samme som vi tenker på når vi ser på forholdet mellom populasjon og utvalg.
Bayesansk tankegang har vært lansert innenfor nevrale nett for å regulere antall noder.
Og så videre. Men fremdeles dreier det seg for en stor del om disjunkte verdener.
Lignende nesten vanntette skott mot statistikken finner en også mot andre dataanalysemiljøer, for eksempel til en viss grad hos kjemometrikerne. En meget dyktig forsker

jeg snakket med forleden, spesialist på analyse av matvarer, drev daglig med PLSregresjon i forskjellige varianter, men hadde aldri hørt om vanlig multippel regresjon.

Kanskje er det riktig at vi bør prise mangfoldet. Men for bevisste brukere av metoder

må situasjonen være meget forvirrende: Er det tilfredsstillende at mitt valg av metode til
å analysere mine konkrete data skal avhenge i så sterk grad av hvilken skole jeg har lært
metodikk fra? Selvfølgelig kan det sies at en intelligent bruk av metoder alltid vil føre
til fornuftige konklusjoner. Likevel mener jeg at det ligger en meget stor utfordring til
statistikere i hele denne problemstillingen: Ikke nødvendigvis bare i det å markedsføre

våre egne metoder, men vel så verdifullt i å videreutvikle det som kanskje når alt
kommer til alt det viktigste i vår kultur: Vi har i den matematiske statistikken kombinert
med dagens muligheter for simuleringer kraftige metoder til å vurdere metoder. Dette
arsenalet er langt fra godt nok utnyttet.

Erling Sverdrup

En nestor blant norske statistikere, professor dr. philos.Erling Sverdrup er død, 77 år

:

Sverdrup var aktuarstudent da Tyskland overfalt Norge 9. april 1940. Han meldte seg

ammel.

samme dag frivillig til tjeneste i Den norske generalstaben og fulgte denne nordover under
hele felttoget. Han var en viktig person i den lille gruppen som sørget for sikker
chifferkorrespondanse mellom de ulike norske avdelingene oggeneralstaben. Da kampene i
Nord-Norge opphørte tok Sverdrup seg over grensen til Finland hvor han ble chiffermann
hos den norske militærattasjeen i Helsingfors. Deretter ble han i tur og orden beordret til
Stockholm, Moskva, Tokyo og Washington DC for å organisere det norske chiffersambandet.
I mars 1942 kom Sverdrup til London hvor han arbeidet i den norske chiffertjenesten.
Oktober 1944 ble han sendt til det frigjorte Finnmark, der han tjenestegjorde inntil Tysklands
kapitulasjon.

Sverdrup fullførte sin aktuarembetseksamen ved Universitetet i Oslo høsten 1945.
Etter en kortere tid som sjef for forsvarets sentrale chifferkontor, ble han i 1946 knyttet til det
økonometriske miljøet rundt Frisch og Haavelmo. Dette fikk avgjørende betydning for hans
interesse for statistikkfaget. Sverdrup ble ansatt som vitenskapelig assistent, og eneste

vitenskapelige ansatte, ved Forsikringsteknisk seminar i 1948. I 1949-50 var han Rockefellerstipendiat, først ved University of California, Berkeley, deretter ved University of Chicago
og Columbia University, New York. Han tok sin doktorgrad i 1952 med en avhandling om et
desisjonsteoretisk emne, og ble i 1953 utnevnt til professor i forsikringsmatematikk med
matematisk statistikk ved Universitetet i Oslo.

Sverdrup gikk straks i gang med å revidere studiet. Statistikk var den gang ikke
etablert som eget fag ved noe norsk universitet. Sverdrup valgte derfor å konsentrere seg om
arbeidet med å bygge opp et moderne studietilbudi statistikk for aktuarer og andre
realfagsstudenter. Matematisk statistikk ble etablert som bifag i 1955 og som hovedfag i
1956. Deretter reorganiserte Sverdrup undervisningen i forsikringsmatematikk slik at denne
ble basert på den nye grunnutdanningeni statistikk. Han tok så opp arbeidet med å skrive

egnede lærebøker. Hans to-binds verk, "Lov og tilfeldighet", har hatt meget stor betydning
for utviklingen av statistikkfaget i Norge. Bøkene er senere revidert, oversatt til engelsk og
gitt ut på et internasjonalt forlag.

Sverdrup hari sine vitenskapelige arbeider særlig behandlet de generelle prinsippene
for konstruksjon av statistiske metoder. Han har blant annet levert viktige arbeider om
optimale statistiske tester og multiple metoder. Han har også levert betydningsfulle bidrag
innen livsforsikringsmatematikk og statistisk forøpsanalyse. Hans arbeider i forlgpsanalyse
har vært et utgangspunkt for den sterke norske og skandinaviske tradisjonen på dette feltet.
Sverdrup ble innvalgt som medlem av Det Norske Videnskaps-Akademiet i 1954, og
av Det Kgl. Norske Videnskabers Selskab i 1981. Han var norsk medlem av International
Statistical Institute, Fellow av Institute of Mathematical Statistics og æresmedlem av Den
norske aktuarforening og Norsk statistisk forening. Sverdrup var norsk redaktør av
Skandinavisk Aktuarietidskrift 1968-82 og formanni Forsikringsrådet 1970-74.
Sverdrup var en inspirerende lærer med utpreget evne til å se de viktige prinsipielle
sidene av et problem uten å tape seg bort i matematiske detaljer. Han hadde sterke meninger
om statistikkens idemessige grunnlag, men var likevel åpen for nye utviklingstendenser i
faget. Han var en intellektuell i ordets beste forstand med store kunnskaper også utenfor sitt
eget fag. Sverdrup kunne i offisielle sammenhenger virke reservert og formell. Men de av oss
som var så heldige å bli nærmere kjent med han, lærte å kjenne et varmt og levende menneske
med humoristisk sans og interesse for andres ve og vel. Minnet om et fint menneske og en

- imponerende karriere vil leve lenge blant oss.

Ørnulf Borgan

Fra: Nordberg, R.[ed.] (1989) Twelve Contributions to a Tradition in Statistics and

Erling Sverdrup—Portrait of a Pioneer in Actuarial and

Statistical Science in Norway
RAGNAR NORBERG
University of Oslo

Erling Sverdrup, mathematical statistician, born in Bergen, Norway, February 23. 1917,
son of Georg Johan, Prof., and Gunvor (Gregusson) Sverdrup. Actuarian, Univ. of Oslo

1945, Dr. Philos. 1952. Statistical consultant at Inst. of Economics, Univ. of Oslo, 1946-48,
research assistant at Inst. of Insurance Mathematics, Univ. of Oslo, 1948-53, professor of

actuarial mathematics and mathematical statistics at Inst. of Mathematics, Univ. of Oslo

1953-84, senior research scholarship, Norwegian Research Council for Science and the
Humanities, 1984-87. Rockefeller Foundation fellow at Univ. of California at Berkeley,

Univ. of Chicago, and Columbia Univ., 1949-50, Visiting professor at Columbia Univ.,
New York, 1963-64 and at Aarhus Univ., 1969, Research officer at the Central Bureau of

Statistics 1977-78. Fellow of the Institute of Mathematical Statistics, member of the Royal
Norwegian Academy of Sciences and the International Statistical Institute. Author of

“Laws and Chance Variations”, North-Holland, 1967. Research on sampling distributions

in multivariate analysis; developed theorem on limit distributions of continuous functions
of random variables; minimax procedures in statistical inference and statistical prediction;

relationship between unbiased (similar) test procedures and conditional testing for general

exponential classes of distributions, generalizing and reformulating Neyman—Pearson’s
theory; estimation and test procedures for forces of decrement and transfers in statistical
follow-up studies. Home addresss: Olav Kyrres gate 4, N-0273, Oslo 2, Norway.

This text, taken from Who’s Who in the World, updated and slightly extended, is, presumably,
an adequate mention of the world citizen Erling Sverdrup. To a readership of statisticians
much has to be added, however.
To begin with the list of exclusive memberships, Sverdrup was elected honorary member of
the Norwegian Statistical Society at the Society’s general assembly in Bergen, 1987. The event
marked the 70th anniversary of Sverdrup’s birth in the very same city and was an expression of
acknowledgement of his endeavours to build statistics in Norway.
Sverdrup is a member of the Norwegian Polytechnical Society and, of course, the Norwegian Society of Actuaries, where he has played a prominent part as lecturer and discussant.

In his lecture "Scientific requirements in the actuarial profession” [13] presented to the
Society on the occasion of its 50th anniversary in 1954, the then newly appointed professor

outlined his vision of a modern actuarial study programme. Its primary objective is not to
teach the students how to run the daily routine work in an insurance company, but to educate
them: make them firmly acquainted with the mathematical-statistical basis of actuarial science
and introduce them to scientific method through one year’s work on a thesis, thus enabling

them to formulate and solve new practical actuarial problems currently arising in a rapidly
developing society. This talk gave rise to discussions through many years, a committee report

and new meetings. A quarter of a century later, in 1979, the programme was put into effect,

with full support from the Society. Already as Sverdrup entered upon his duties as professor,

he made important steps to build a scientifically based actuarial education by designing
Courses in insurance mathematics founded on modern probability theory and statistics. The

forerunners of [25] and [26] were decades ahead of contemporary textbooks in insurance

At pensionable age, Sverdrup retired from the senior scholarship he held in the three last
years of his working career. Thus, his present position is pensioner, Professor emeritus at the

University of Oslo. None the less, the present survey of his works and activities can only be
provisional: it seems that Sverdrup for the time being is in one of those periods of feverish

enthusiasm which—according to himself—have been characteristic of his research work.

The scientific production that grew out of these periods is considerable—in the true sense of
the word. Actually, Sverdrup has been reticent in publishing his results in the traditional form

of papers in journals.

A number of significant contributions have remained unpublished or

they have been placed in memoranda, lecture notes or textbooks without visible claims of
originality. A typical case is the chapter on moving averages in [16]-[17], which gives precise
mathematical formulation of, and solves problems related to, the graduation of vital rates,

time series, etc. Another example is the volume of lecture notes [25], which treats problems of

life insurance in a probabilistic setting that accommodates studies of the risk associated with
the single policy and the entire portfolio. The text includes also an original and clarifying
treatment of safety loadings in life insurance and their return by bonus allotment schemes.

Also [27] and [29] ought to be mentioned. In a chat I once had with Severdrup on the publishor-perish-syndrome, he uttered: “If one should write something, it must be to express what

One has most at heart”. All his writings are well reasoned, with a clear concern and message.
A special commitment can be traced in the works on statistical decision theory. The thesis

for the doctorate [9] states and proves a number of results on admissibility and minimaxity,
both with and without use of least favourable distributions. Some of them have acquired a firm

Position as key theorems in the field. In the introduction to the thesis Sverdrup states that
some of the results were in the air at that time, but on the whole this work must be recognized
as an important early contribution to the development of the general theory on admissible and

minimax procedures. Its import is also due to the efforts made to find admissible minimax
solutions to certain prominent testing problems in normal models, which added knowledge

about the feasibility of the minimax criterion.

Paper [11] marks a turning point in that Sverdrup here gives his last contribution to minimax
theory and commences his concentrated studies in the foundations of the Neyman-Pearson

theory of testing statistical hypotheses. In [11] he streamlines and extends the theory on the

mathematical side. An elegant proof, based on analytic continuation, is given for a generalized
version of Neyman and Pearson's result about the construction of similar tests, covering
both

discrete and continuous distributions. The role of similarity as a necessary condition for

unbiasedness is made lucid, and the classical Neyman-Pearson lemma is presented in a general

form for unbiased tests of hypotheses in the multiparameter exponential family
of

distributions.

Later works ([19], [27], [34], [35], [37], [38], [55]) clarify the practical interpretations of the
Neyman-Pearson theory and relate it to general scientific method. In particular the interpretation of the role of the null hypothesis in the context of multiple testing has brought relief

to statisticians. Here are some excerpts from the philosophy:
— The statistical method should be derived from a model embodying all relevant a priori
knowledge/insight about the mechanisms that generate the data. The properties of a
method can be assessed only when it has been specified in advance which conclusion is to

be drawn for each possible outcome of the observations ([16]-[17], Chapters I and VI).

The statistical method should be judged from the characteristics of the performance

function. Its behaviour must be explored for any distribution which cannot a priori be

excluded.
—A

statistician who believes that he looks at the observations before he constructs the

method, hides part of his model in his subconscious mind. It is indeed impossible to look

at the observations in advance. It would be pulling yourself up by your hair ([27], pp.
44-47).
— It is not contradictory to test null-hypotheses which are known to be false and would
have been rejected anyhow if the number of observations were large enough. The null-

hypothesis should properly be called the null-state. It is chosen, not because we have
any a priori confidence in it, or are interested in the truth of it, but because we are
interested in certain effects which are contrasts to the hypothesis. The test is just a
clearance test, performed initially to determine whether or not to search further for

significant contrasts/departures from the hypothesis (see [38]).
— Alone and by itself the null-hypothesis gives no guidance to the choice of a test. The
purpose of the test and hence criterion for designing it becomes clear only when one has

specified the alternatives it is to be tested against (see [35], [38], [55]).
Besides the influential works in statistical decision theory, Sverdrup’s early contributions to
modern life history analysis are recognized as the origin of a strong tradition in actuarial and

statistical research in Scandinavia. Key references are [18] and [21].
The principles of statistical inference that have been so vigorously advanced by Sverdrup,
with emphasis on the model as the basis for analysis of data, are apparently inspired by, and

carry forth the style of scientific thinking that prevailed in the school of Ragnar Frisch, Trygve

Haavelmo, Olav Reiersøl and other influential Norwegian econometricians.
The laconic formulations in Who’s Who in the World cover up the circumstance that
Sverdrup was appointed professor of statistics at a time when this academic discipline was in a
state of infancy in Norway, and the building of an educational system and an environment of

Statistics was a great challenge. Sverdrup seized the psychological moment—and was seized by

it and by the following three decades of steady commitments, with numerous initiatives for the
good of the statistics profession. The archives of the University of Oslo contain innumerable
documents signed by him: strategic plans for education and research, study programmes
and
curricula, appraisals of applicants for positions, evaluations of theses and doctoral disserta-

tions, reports from working parties and committees, and records of all kinds of run-of-the-mill
matters. These documents maintain reflected points of view concerning the university’s role in

society, advocating the unity of education and science and freedom of research—in particular

the rights of the researcher to choose topics of study and publish results—freed from the —
necessity of producing short-term results for external contracts. Already as a research assistant

he became head of "Forsikringsteknisk seminar”, the forerunner of the Section of Actuarial
Mathematics and Statistics. He was head of the Section through the bulk of his time as

professor, and has also served
as head of the Department of Mathematics and chairman of the
Faculty Committee of Budgeting and Planning. In administrative work he combined thorough
insight in procedural matters with the best of political calculation, always soberminded, never
hiding anything.

In addition to his administrative work-load at the university, Sverdrup was Norwegian
editor of the Scandinavian Actuarial Journal 1968-82, chairman of the Norwegian Council of
Insurance Supervision 1970-74, and chairman of the Norwegian Mathematics Council

1974-76 (vice-chairman 1973-74).

Alsoin his teaching Sverdrupis a reflected man. His abilities as a teacher are not only the
result of natural talent, thorough preparation and an excellent grasp of the material. He

regards lecturing as an act of dramatizing the subject. What is hard to convey by the written

During the Second World War Sverdrup devoted himself entirely to the fight for freedom. I
have heard him make one statement in public on this stern period of his life: *I usually deduct
five years from my age”. I consider it appropriate to add some information I have obtained
from a first-hand source, Karl Borch, whose career has striking parallels to that of Sverdrup.
They were fellow students, and both served as intelligence officers in the Free Norwegian

Forces during the war, thereby suffering a five-year-long break in their studies. They resumed
their studies upon the conclusion of peace, received their actuarial degrees, and ended up as
professors of insurance (Borch at the Norwegian School of Economics and Business Adminis-

tration, Bergen). In a very personal and frank letter dated 24 November 1986, Borch wrote to

me (translated extract):
In the autumn of 1939 the General Staff organized a course in ciphering at the university,
with the purpose of building a group of cipher experts who could be called up when needed.
I was recruited by actuary Per Gotaas, and I think he also was the one who recruited
Sverdrup.

On the night of 9 April 1940, Sverdrup reported for duty and followed the General Staff
northwards. For this I admire Sverdrup. He knew immediately—with an unfailing instinct—
what was the right thing to do. Sverdrup was exempted from military duty for medical
reasons and, therefore, was not under mobilization orders. He nevertheless volunteered.

He was sent to Helsinki, Moscow and Tokyo to organize the cipher service at the
Norwegian legations there. Then he was ordered to Washington and, in 1942, to London,
where he was in charge of a special military cipher service. In 1944 he was sent back to the
northern part of Norway after it had been liberated by the Red Army, and he served there
until the German capitulation. He had then attained the rank of captain.
In the Norwegian Who’s Who Sverdrup makes no mention of his war effort. I don’t know
why he has been so reticent and stayed in the background. I think you should pass on some

of what I have told here to throw light on the character of Erling Sverdrup. His merits in the
struggle for freedom were known to many, but most of them have passed away.

Karl Borch died on 2 December 1986, one week after he wrote this letter.

Other personal characteristics of Erling Sverdrup are well known to his acquaintances. The
following opening phrases seem worn-out when appearing in piece-goods Western novels, but
here they are almost perfect: "The steel-grey steady gaze tells you that this man will not give an
inch. Six foot tall, slender—almost lean...” He is easily recognized in street life, striding
along at an impressive pace, wearing a hat, an overcoat with flowing tails and a well-filled

briefcase. One of the last indomitable pedestrians in the era of mass motorism (he owned a car
in those days when that was a mark of distinction and still fun).
His sparsely equipped bachelor’s lair houses a huge book collection (no TV set). His

favourite reading seems to be history, politics and biographies, reflecting a deep social
awareness and interest in individual fates, and also science, in particular natural sciences and

their history.

’

At times Sverdrup disappears from town life. In case of emergency one can then with
probable success write to Kalvetreet, N-5850 Balestrand. Here, in the West Country, is his
cabin and second home, where he works and rests in solitude. Food is provided by fishing in



Erling Sverdrup

the sea and in lakes and creeks. His reports on the culinary pleasures of smoked salmon, boiled
coalfish with melted butter, fish soups, etc., are most engaging. Then there is that cuttlefish in
ink sauce...

He is a keen trout fisherman. In younger days he used to raom in the wilderness with a
minimum of equipment: a fishing rod and a frying pan, often no hiking tent. A blend of the

refined and the primitive.

It is hard to trace the caveman disposition in the everyday-life appearance of Sverdrup:
attentive, correct—in official matters rather formal, usually wearing a suit (there must be at
least two of them). Some signs of his character are the great demands he makes on himself, his
total reliance on his own strength, and his insisting on complete personal responsibility. On
closer acquaintance you meet a considerate colleague and friend—and a good mixer: a source
of ideas and engagements, a gift for story-telling combined with observant listening, a goodnatured sense of humour with no strain of mockery, and a smile which is a miraculous
collection of horizontal wrinkles.

Inevitably Sverdrup created a tradition in actuarial and statistical science at the University
of Oslo and in Norway. It is a historical circumstance that he was summoned to leadership in a
young academic field, where individual achievements necessarily would be decisive. How-

ever, the results are entirely attributable to the strong efforts of this self-sacrificing organizer
and honest scientist. Younger Norwegian statisticians, who have entered into a well established educational system and proceeded in a rich environment of statistical operations under

stable social conditions in peace-time, must take off their hats to the remarkable person and
career described here.

A 200-year-old probability theory could help to avoid some of the miscarriages of justice
that embarrass the courts and have undermined public trust in the judicial system

Improving the odds on justice?
Robert Matthews

ALL over Britain, juries of ordinary people
regularly listen to evidence in courts of law.
It can be anything from a child's statement
alleging ritual abuse to the probability of a
DNA match. Somehow, the jury has to pull
the evidence together, assess its relative
merits and decide on the probability of guilt
of the accused.
It is a notoriously difficult task. When
mistakes are made, the results can be catastrophic. Yet few members of juries, barristers, or even judges realise that techniques
designed to solve their age-old problem
already exist. Earlier this week, the Royal
Statistical Society met to discuss these
techniques. Known collectively as Bayesian
inference theory, they could revolutionise
the way that juries reach their decision—
and dramatically reduce the risk of miscarriages of justice.
The techniques are based on principles
first published by Thomas Bayes, an 18thcentury cleric, who worked out how new
evidence can modify the odds for a particular hypothesis. Although they are now
more than 200 years old, their importance
for the law was highlighted last December
by an important Court of Appeal case in
which Bayesian inference theory helped
secure a retrial of a convicted rapist.
In 1990, Andrew Deen was sentenced to
16 years in jail at Manchester Crown Court

fingerprint of samples found at the scene of

large a figure, if correct, “approximates
pretty well to certainty”.

judges made it clear that both the forensic

Bent Natvig og Tore Nordseth: Sannsynlighetsregning. Etterutdanningshefte. Tilvalgsstoff i 1MA.

Aschehaug 1993.

Vi som underviser i begynnerkurseri statistikk på universiteter og høgskoler, har et stort handicap i
forhold til våre kolleger matematikeme: Stort sett vet studentene ingenting om faget vårt på forhånd.
Heldigvis begynner dette å bedre seg noe; det tas inn etterhvert noe stoff i sannsynlighetsregning og
statistikk under matematikk-undervisningen i den videregående skolen. Dette heftet er ifølge
omslaget beregnet til etterutdanning av lærere, pensum ved lærerhøgskoler, tilvalgsstoff for eleveri
1MA, samt til idébank og repetisjon. Som tittelen sier, konsentrerer det seg om

sannsynlighetsregningen.

På mange måter er sannsynlighetsregningen den mest utakknemlige delen av faget vårt å undervise i:
Det blir ofte til en oppterping av terninger, kortspill og trekking av fargete kuler, og studenter og
elever mister lett motivasjonen. Vi finner både kort, terninger og kuler i Natvig og Nordseth's hefte
også, men mange gode virkemidler er tatt i bruk for å gjøre stoffet mer interessant: Aktuelle spill som
lotto, rikstoto og tipping er benyttet i rikelig monn med realistiske bilder av kuponger som figurer.
Historiske bemerkninger er med på å krydre stoffet. Ikke minst er bruken av aktuelle fornavn
(Vegard, Bjørn og Vladimir...) i oppgavene morsom.

På ett punkt har imidlertid forfatterne blitt innhentet av uforutsette begivenheter når det gjelder disse
navnene: Bruken av ‘Johan Jørgen" sammen med andre politikemavn i oppgavene 1.8 og 5.3 er ikke
lenger fullt så festlig. På mange måter er det smakløst av en bøkanmelder å trekke frem dette poenget,
men når det først er gjort, er jeg fristet til å trekke det litt etter hårene: I de siste kapitlene i heftet
anvendes sannsynlighetsregningen bl.a. på pålitelighetsanalyse-beregninger. Spørsmålet man ofte
sitter igjen med etter slike regninger, er: Vil ikke de katastrofene som skje, ofte komme der man
overhodet ikke venter dem på forhånd, kanskje man ikke tenker på muligheten engang?
Fra disse kjetterske tankene om sannsynlighetsregningen, la oss gå tilbake til heftet: Stoffet er fint og
enkelt presentert, og utvalget av oppgaver er rikelig og illustrerende. Jeg har lyst til å trekke frem én
oppgave: I oppgave 1.10 gis en enkel og klar fremstilling av Marilyn von Savant's TV-spill, som har
skapt het debatt i mange akademiske miljøer, er tidligere (mars 1993) diskutert her i TG av Steinar
Engen, og også i enda mer seriøse tidsskrifter (American Statistician, 45, 284-289 (1991); 45, 387-388
(1991); 47, 82-83 (1993) osv.)

Av eksemplene i heftet kan nevnes brosystemet i Eksempel 7.5 med 5 komponenter: 1 + 4
i parallell med 2 + 5 og med komponent 3 som en bro mellom de to delene. I heftet blir
sannsynligheten for at et slikt system skal funksjonere, beregnet relativt enkelt ved først å betinge med
hensyn på om komponent 3 funksjonerer eller ikke. Jeg skulle imidlertid gjerne se den gymnasiasten
som ville klare en slik oppgave uten å få noe hint!

For de sterkeste elevene tror jeg dette hefte vil virke både motiverende og stimulerende, og jeg tror
mange vil føle at de finner mye interessant. Jeg er redd endel svake elever vil få vanskeligheter med
den uunngåelig tunge notasjonen som sannsynlighetsregningen ofte fører med seg. Ellers er det
vanskelig å si hvordan å si hvordan et slikt hefte vil virke i praksis uten å ha eksponert elever i den
akteuelle gruppen for det.

Et lite kritisk punkt til slutt: Både på omslagssiden og i forordet snakkes det om hvor viktig det for
eksempel er i politikken å kunne vurdere risiko og gjøre riktige valg. Det finnes noen få eksempler og
oppgaver i heftet som kan knyttes opp mot denne alvorlige motivasjonen, men de har ikke noen særlig
fremtredende plass.

I alt, imidlertid: Heftet anbefales!
Inge Helland

Medlemskap i Norsk Statistisk Forening

Kontigenten i foreningen er på kr. 150 pr. år (kr. 90 for 1993) for regulære medlemmer og kr.
50 for studenter (kr. 30 for 1993). Korrespondanse om medlemskap og innbetaling av

kontigent skal skje til foreningens kasserer:

Norsk Statistisk Forening v/Eva Seim
Statistisk avd. Nycomed A.S.
p.b. 4220 Torshov
0401 OSLO

Innbetalinger skal gjøres på foreningens postgirokonto 0802 5 15 10 55. Medlemmer som er
usikre på når de betalte kontigent sist, vil nederst til høyre på adressseddelen for Tilfeldig Gang
finne angitt hvilket år siste betaling er registrert for. Vær imidlertid oppmerksom på at
adressesedlene for bladet ligger noe etter ajourføringen av medlemsregisteret, slik at nylig

foretatte innbetalinger ikke vil være tatt med her.

Nye medlemmer melder seg til kassereren under adressen oppgitt ovenfor. Slike medlemmer
får automatisk tilsendt Tilfeldig Gang, med forbehold om at det tar noe tid før tilføyelser og
forandringer i medlemsregisteret gjenspeiler seg i adressesedlene til bladet.
Adresseforandringer meldes også til kassereren. Ved utsendelsen av Tilfeldig Gang benyttes
det kopier av det vanlige medlemsregisteret, så adresseforandringer behøver ikke meldes
separat til bladet.
